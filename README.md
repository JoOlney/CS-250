# CS-250
Software Development Lifecycle: SNHU 2026

This portfolio submission documents the culminating sprint review and retrospective for the SNHU Travel application project completed at ChadaTech. The project served as a pilot for transitioning from a traditional waterfall development model to a Scrum-Agile approach. In my role as Scrum Master, I facilitated the sprint ceremonies, removed blockers, and ensured that Agile principles were applied consistently throughout the project. 
The SNHU Travel application allows users to search for and book vacation packages. Development was carried out in iterative sprints, with each sprint delivering a working increment of the product — beginning with a basic search feature and expanding into filtering, booking, and mobile-friendly capabilities in later sprints.

Interpreting user needs starts with listening — in Agile, that means working closely with the Product Owner to translate what customers describe in plain language into structured, actionable user stories. For the SNHU Travel project, a user story like "As a traveler, I want to search for vacation packages based on budget" gave the development team a clear, testable goal tied directly to a real user need. User stories are valuable because they keep the focus on the outcome the user wants rather than the technical implementation details. They also include acceptance criteria, which define exactly when a story is "done" — removing ambiguity and reducing the chance that a feature is built incorrectly. Creating user stories forced me to think about the system from the outside in, from the user's perspective, rather than from the inside out starting with code.

My approach to developing programs is iterative and incremental. Rather than designing an entire system upfront and building it all at once, I prefer to deliver small, working pieces that can be tested and validated by stakeholders before moving forward. In the SNHU Travel project, this meant building a basic search feature first, confirming it met the acceptance criteria, then layering filtering and booking capabilities on top in subsequent sprints. This approach surfaced problems early — when a mid-sprint API integration issue emerged, it affected only one sprint rather than derailing an entire project. In future work, I plan to incorporate daily stand-ups to maintain team transparency, sprint retrospectives to continuously improve the team's process, and a well-maintained product backlog to ensure that priorities stay aligned with user and business needs. I also want to continue using digital task boards (Jira or Trello) to give every team member real-time visibility into what is in progress, what is blocked, and what is complete.

Being a good team member in software development means showing up prepared, communicating proactively, and taking ownership of both your individual work and the team's shared goals. In the Scrum framework, this is made concrete through daily stand-ups: every team member answers what they worked on yesterday, what they plan to do today, and what is blocking them. This transparency prevents problems from being hidden until they become crises. As Scrum Master, I also learned that good team membership means creating the conditions for others to do their best work — removing obstacles quickly, protecting the team from unnecessary interruptions, and fostering an environment where raising a problem is seen as a contribution rather than a failure. Good team members in software development are collaborative, honest about their progress, and genuinely invested in the team's success rather than only their own tasks.
